Dementia & Memory Care

Specialized care for the person — not just the diagnosis.

Dementia is not one disease, and dementia care isn't one approach. Our caregivers are trained in the specific behaviors, communication strategies, and safety needs that come with Alzheimer's, Lewy body, vascular, and frontotemporal dementia — with RN oversight throughout.

Memory care done right

Dementia care is a skill. Our caregivers train for it before they ever take a case.

Every Carevider caregiver assigned to a dementia case completes 24+ hours of specialized training beyond their standard certification — covering communication, redirection, safety, and the four most common dementia types. Our RN care managers build the care plan around the specific stage and behaviors, not a one-size-fits-all checklist.

What dementia-specialized care includes

  • Stage-appropriate activities and routines
  • Validation therapy (not arguing with reality)
  • Redirection during agitation
  • Wandering prevention and home safety
  • Meaningful engagement (music, photos, hands-on)
  • Bathing approaches for resistant clients
  • Family caregiver coaching
  • Coordination with neurologist or geriatrician

When to consider memory care placement instead

Home care works for most. Sometimes a memory care community is the right answer.

About 70% of our dementia clients are cared for at home through the full progression of the disease. The other 30% reach a point where a secured memory care community is safer or more sustainable. We offer both — and we'll tell you honestly which is the right fit for your situation.

  • Frequent nighttime wandering despite supervision
  • Aggression that puts the family at risk
  • Around-the-clock supervision needs the family can't fund
  • Loss of swallowing safety
  • Caregiver burnout that's no longer manageable
  • Geographic isolation
  • Geographic isolation without a backup network

Common questions

Dementia care questions families ask.

How is dementia care different from regular home care?
Dementia care requires specific communication techniques (validation rather than correction), structured routines that reduce anxiety, behavior strategies for sundowning and agitation, and home safety adapted for confusion and wandering. A standard caregiver may have none of this training. At Carevider, every caregiver assigned to a dementia case completes 24+ hours of dementia-specific training beyond the state requirements, and an RN builds the behavioral care plan.
Can someone with dementia stay at home for the entire disease?
For about 70% of our dementia clients, yes — they remain at home through end-of-life with progressively increasing care. The factors that allow this are: caregiver consistency (same faces matter), home safety modifications, family support, financial resources, and absence of severe behavioral issues. Some families reach a point where memory care placement becomes safer or more affordable — we help with both paths.
What if my parent gets agitated or refuses care?
This is one of the most common reasons families call us. Resistance to care is rarely about the care itself — it's usually about loss of control, embarrassment, or misinterpreting the situation. Our caregivers are trained to slow down, use non-threatening body language, build the task into a familiar routine, and step away when needed. Resistant clients almost always accept care once trust is established, typically within 1–3 weeks.
Should we tell them they have dementia? They keep forgetting.
In early stages, yes — they have a right to participate in decisions about their own life. In middle and later stages, repeating the diagnosis often causes fresh grief without any benefit, because they won't retain the information. Our RN care managers coach families on the validation approach: meeting the person where they are emotionally, rather than correcting their reality. This reduces distress for everyone.
What does dementia home care cost in San Diego?
Dementia care is priced the same as our standard home care — $38/hr hourly, $395/day live-in, $28/hr for 24-hour shift care — with no surcharge for the specialty training. Most dementia clients start with 4–8 hours daily and increase over time. A full 24-hour care plan averages $9,500–$12,000/month depending on configuration.
