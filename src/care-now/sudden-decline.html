Sudden Decline

"Mom seemed fine last month. Now I'm worried."

Sudden changes in eating, energy, memory, or steadiness are rarely "just aging." They're usually a signal — sometimes medical, sometimes environmental, sometimes both. An RN in-home assessment can pinpoint what's actually changed, and what to do next.

Warning signs we hear most

  • Lost weight without trying
  • Sleeping much more — or less
  • Stopped cooking or driving
  • Repeating questions within minutes
  • Mail piling up unopened
  • Bruises with no explanation
What's actually happening

Sudden decline almost always has a cause. Often a fixable one.

Geriatricians group sudden decline into four categories. Most cases involve more than one, and many are reversible if caught early. Our RN assessment screens for all four — and refers out to a physician when warranted.

The four common causes

  • Medical: UTI, dehydration, anemia, thyroid
  • Medication: New Rx, interaction, or missed dose
  • Cognitive: Early dementia, depression, delirium
  • Environmental: Recent loss, isolation, poor nutrition
  • Sensory: Hearing or vision loss limiting engagement
  • Sleep: Apnea, pain, nighttime confusion
  • Pain: Untreated arthritis, neuropathy, dental
  • Social: Death of spouse, friends, or pet

What to ask the doctor before assuming "it's just aging"

A checklist for the next physician visit.

Many sudden declines get dismissed at a 15-minute primary care visit. Bring this list — it covers the most common reversible causes we see in San Diego seniors.

  • Urinalysis to rule out UTI (often presents as confusion)
  • CBC and metabolic panel (anemia, electrolytes, kidney)
  • Thyroid panel (TSH, free T4)
  • Vitamin B12 and D levels
  • Medication review — any new Rx in last 6 months?
  • Cognitive screen (MoCA or Mini-Cog)
  • Depression screen (PHQ-9)
  • Hearing and vision check

If the doctor says "it's just getting older"

Ask specifically: "What did you rule out?" Sudden decline over weeks or a few months is rarely normal aging. If you're not getting answers, our RN can do a fresh in-home assessment and help you advocate for the right workup.

Common questions

When something just feels off.

How do I tell the difference between normal aging and sudden decline?
Normal aging is gradual — small changes over years. Sudden decline is measurable over weeks or a few months: noticeably less appetite, new confusion, withdrawal from familiar activities, unexplained weight loss, or a fall they don't remember. If you can point to a specific time window when things shifted, that's a signal worth investigating — not waiting on.
Could a UTI really cause confusion in a senior?
Yes — and it's one of the most missed diagnoses in geriatrics. In older adults, urinary tract infections often present as sudden confusion, agitation, or "personality change" before any of the classic urinary symptoms appear. A simple urinalysis can rule it out in a day. We routinely flag this for the physician when we see new-onset confusion during an RN assessment.
What if my parent insists nothing is wrong?
Very common. We frame the RN visit as a "wellness check" or "second opinion" rather than an evaluation. Most seniors agree once it's positioned as helpful information rather than judgment. If they refuse outright, we coach the family on how to reframe the conversation — and we can also do a "side door" visit by accompanying you on a routine errand or meal.
Do I need a doctor's referral for the RN assessment?
No. Our RN in-home assessment is a private-pay clinical evaluation, not a Medicare service, so no referral is required. The assessment is free and comes with a written report you can share with the primary care doctor, a geriatrician, or family members involved in the decision.
How fast can the RN come out?
Most San Diego County RN assessments are scheduled within 2–4 business days. For urgent situations — recent fall, acute confusion, safety concerns — we typically schedule within 24 hours. Call our 24/7 line if it feels urgent; the on-call RN can also do a phone triage to help you decide whether to head to the ER first.

If something feels off, trust that instinct.

Adult children are usually the first to notice sudden decline — and they're usually right. A free RN assessment will tell you what's actually happening, and what to do next.
