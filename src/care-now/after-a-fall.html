After a Fall

One fall changes everything. The next one is preventable.

After a senior falls, the risk of falling again within 12 months doubles. The good news: most repeat falls are preventable with the right combination of home safety, mobility support, and trained hands close by. We've built a fall-recovery protocol around exactly that.

Why falls are bigger than they look

A single fall is rarely just a single fall.

In adults over 65, falls are the leading cause of injury-related death and the most common reason for emergency room visits. But the bigger issue is what comes after: loss of confidence, reduced activity, muscle loss, and a measurably higher chance of falling again. Breaking that cycle requires action in the first 30 days.

What we address after a fall

  • Recovery from the immediate injury
  • Medication review (especially blood pressure, sleep aids)
  • Home safety — rugs, lighting, bathrooms, stairs
  • Vision and hearing check coordination
  • Footwear assessment
  • Mobility aids fitted properly (walker, cane)
  • Strength and balance support
  • Fear-of-falling counseling

San Diego home safety checklist

The 12 hazards we flag in almost every home.

Our RNs have walked through hundreds of San Diego homes after a senior fall. The same dozen issues show up over and over — and most are fixable in a weekend.

  • Loose rugs and runners on tile or wood floors
  • No grab bars in shower or beside the toilet
  • Bathroom mats without non-slip backing
  • Dim or missing nightlights in hallway to bathroom
  • Cords running across walking paths
  • Stairs without sturdy handrails on both sides
  • Pets that weave underfoot (especially small dogs)
  • Outdoor pavers, hose, or step transitions
  • Bed too high or too low to stand from
  • Kitchen items stored above shoulder height
  • Worn slippers or backless footwear
  • Bifocals or progressives on stairs

About the "long lie"

The most dangerous outcome of a fall isn't usually the fall itself — it's lying on the floor for hours before being found. Even uninjured falls can cause dehydration, pressure injuries, or hypothermia within 60–90 minutes. A caregiver presence, medical alert device, or daily check-in protocol dramatically lowers this risk.

Just had a fall?

If there's any chance of a head injury, hip injury, or loss of consciousness, go to the ER first. Then call us to set up recovery support at home.

Common questions

After-a-fall questions families ask.

How likely is another fall after the first one?
Roughly twice as likely within the next 12 months, according to CDC data. The risk is highest in the first 30 days, which is why the immediate post-fall period is so important. With the right home modifications, medication review, and caregiver support, that elevated risk can be substantially reduced — many of our clients go years between falls after one structured intervention.
Does Medicare cover home care after a fall?
Medicare may cover skilled home health (PT, OT, nursing visits) for a limited period if the fall resulted in a qualifying injury and a doctor orders it. Medicare does not cover non-medical home care — bathing, meals, mobility help, overnight presence. Most families combine Medicare home health for the clinical visits with private-pay or LTC-insurance-covered home care for daily support.
What if my parent fell but didn't get hurt — do they still need help?
Yes, often more than people realize. A "minor" fall is the strongest single predictor of a major one. It also frequently signals an underlying issue — medication side effect, blood pressure change, new vision problem, or early balance decline — that won't fix itself. A free RN assessment can identify what caused it before it happens again.
Should we get a medical alert button?
For most seniors at risk of falls, yes — especially if they live alone or are home alone for parts of the day. Modern devices with automatic fall detection and GPS are reliable and inexpensive. They're not a replacement for in-home support if mobility is significantly impaired, but they're an excellent layer of safety. Our RN can recommend specific devices based on the situation.
How do we get them to stop being afraid of falling again?
"Fear of falling" is a clinical phenomenon and it actually increases fall risk because seniors move less, lose strength, and become more unsteady. The most effective response is graded, supervised activity — short walks with a caregiver, sit-to-stand practice, balance work — to rebuild trust in their own body. Most clients regain confidence within 4–8 weeks of consistent support.
