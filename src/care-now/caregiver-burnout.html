Caregiver Burnout & Respite

You can't pour from an empty cup. Let us hold the load.

More than 40% of family caregivers report clinically significant exhaustion within the first year. You don't have to wait until you collapse to ask for help. From 4-hour weekly breaks to full overnight or weekend coverage — respite care is one of the most important investments a family can make.

Common respite patterns

  • Weekly: 4–8 hours, same day each week
  • Workday: 8 hours, Monday–Friday
  • Overnight: Awake or sleep-shift coverage
  • Weekend: Friday evening to Sunday night
  • Vacation: 1–3 weeks of 24/7 coverage
  • End-of-life: Continuous family support
If any of this sounds familiar

Burnout has clear signs. Most family caregivers miss them in themselves.

Caregiver burnout is a clinical condition — not a personal failing. It builds quietly, gets normalized, and is usually obvious to outsiders long before it's obvious to the caregiver. If three or more of these are true for you right now, it's time.

Signs of caregiver burnout

  • You can't remember the last full night of sleep
  • You've stopped seeing friends or skipping your own doctor visits
  • You're irritable in ways you wouldn't have been before
  • You've gained or lost weight without trying
  • You feel resentful, then guilty about feeling resentful
  • Headaches, back pain, or stomach issues are new or worse
  • You've thought "I can't do this anymore" more than once
  • Other family members have told you you look exhausted

What you can actually do with respite

"I don't know what I'd do with the time" is the #1 thing we hear.

Most family caregivers have been on duty so long they've forgotten what they used to enjoy. The first few respite shifts can feel disorienting. That's normal — and it's worth pushing through. Here's what other San Diego families do with theirs.

  • Sleep through the night without listening for them
  • Go to your own medical appointments
  • See friends without rushing back
  • Exercise — even a 30-minute walk counts
  • Cook a meal you actually want to eat
  • Attend a grandchild's game or recital
  • Take a real weekend away, even locally
  • Sit somewhere quiet and just be off-duty

Common questions

Respite questions families ask.

How do I know if I need respite care?
If you're asking the question, the answer is usually yes. Clinically, respite is recommended for any family caregiver providing more than 20 hours of care per week, anyone showing signs of physical or emotional exhaustion, and anyone caring for a loved one with dementia or end-of-life needs. You don't have to be at breaking point to qualify or benefit — most families find earlier respite makes the long-term care journey sustainable.
What's the minimum amount of respite I can book?
Our standard minimum is a 4-hour shift, which is enough for a doctor's appointment, a meal out, or a real break. Most families start with one weekly 4–8 hour shift and adjust from there. There's no monthly minimum and no long-term contract — you can scale up or down based on what you need.
Will my loved one resist having someone new in the house?
Often, yes — at first. The transition is easier when (1) the family caregiver is present for the first 1–2 shifts to make introductions, (2) the caregiver focuses on enjoyable activities rather than personal care initially, and (3) the same caregiver returns each week to build familiarity. Most reluctant clients accept the routine within 2–3 weeks. Our RN can coach you through the introduction.
Can I get overnight or weekend respite?
Yes — these are some of our most-requested respite patterns. Overnight respite (typically 8pm–8am) lets you sleep through the night. Weekend respite (Friday evening through Sunday night) gives you a true break, often paired with a getaway. Vacation respite of 1–3 weeks is also common — we set up full 24/7 coverage with an RN check-in midway through.
Does insurance or Medicare cover respite care?
Medicare generally does not cover non-medical respite care, with one exception: Medicare hospice benefits include up to 5 days of inpatient respite per benefit period. Long-term care insurance does typically cover respite — we verify and bill it directly. VA Aid & Attendance can also offset respite costs for eligible veterans. We help families navigate all three funding paths at no cost.
